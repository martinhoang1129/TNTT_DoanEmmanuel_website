St. Athanasius of Alexandria, known as the "Father of Orthodoxy," was the 4th-century bishop who served as the primary defender of the deity of Christ against the Arian heresy. Most famous for his role at the Council of Nicaea and his defining work \*On the Incarnation\*, he spent seventeen years in exile across five separate banishments for refusing to compromise on the doctrine that the Son is of the same substance as the Father. Beyond his theological resilience, summarized by the phrase \*Athanasius contra mundum\* ("Athanasius against the world"), he is historically significant for being the first to formally list the 27 books of the New Testament canon used today.
